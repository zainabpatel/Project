BLACK VOICES

Celebrating Black women in STEM

Born in Roanoke, Virginia, US, Henrietta Lacks was a victim of medical negligence and exploitation. While undergoing radium treatments for her cervical cancer, a sample of her cancer cells taken from a biopsy by Dr Howard Jones were used in a tissue lab without her knowledge or consent. Although not a scientist, her cells are crucial to the scientific research and disease prevention community.

The cells of Henrietta Lacks became the first immortalised cell line in history named ‘HeLa’ cells. Her tumour cells doubled every 20-24 hours and have been used globally to study the effects of toxins, viruses, various drugs, and vaccines such as the polio vaccine.

Henrietta Lacks died on October 4, 1951 aged only 31. The Lacks family were only made aware of the use of her cells until 1975 and were never compensated for their extraction or use./p>

The Henrietta Lacks Foundation was established in 2010 by Rebecca Skloot, author of The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks. Their aim is to bring to light the contributions of biological materials in scientific research, issues related to consent and disparities in access to health care for minorities and underserved communities.

Henrietta Lacks1920 • Biology • US

Mathematician and US Naval Weapons technologist Gladys West is best known for her important work in developing the Geographical Positioning System (GPS).

West earned a full scholarship to Virginia State University after graduating at the top of her class at high school. Later in her career, Dr West spent 42 years working on the naval base in Virginia, where she was one of four Black employees and the second ever Black woman ever hired. She then worked as the project manager for the Seasat radar altimetry project, the first satellite able to remotely sense oceans. She was integral in researching Pluto’s motion relative to Neptune and used this to work on a geodetic model of Earth, which led to the first GPS orbit. This involved the planning and execution of complex algorithms and a huge amount of data analysis.

The peak of Gladys West’s career coincided with the Civil Rights movement led by figures such as Martin Luther King and Malcolm X, however, she was cautious with her involvement due to her governmental job.

Dr West retired in 1998 and pursued a PhD despite health problems including a stroke and a breast cancer diagnosis. West was conducted into the Air Force Space and Missile Pioneers Hall of Fame in December 2018. Her work is highlighted in Margot Lee Shetterly's book Hidden Figures: The Story of the African-American Women Who Helped Win the Space Race, which was later developed into a film in 2016.

Gladys West1930 • Mathematics • US

Dr. Shirley Jackson was the first African-American woman to have earned a doctorate in the field of physics from MIT. She spent many years of her career researching the idea of particle physics. However, she changed her research area when joining AT & T Bell Laboratories whereby she conducted many experiments, often focusing on electronic properties of two-dimensional condensed matter systems.

During her time at Bell Laboratories, she made outstanding discoveries for which she has been credited for. Jackson was acknowledged for her research that allowed technology to flourish, particularly the invention of caller ID and call waiting alongside portable fax, touch tone telephone, solar cells, and fiber optic cables.

In 1994, Jackson received a call from Bill Clinton. He offered her the opportunity to be the chair of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission in which she developed and assessed risks of the nuclear power plants.

Jackson is currently the 18th President of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute – the oldest technology research institute in the US.

Shirley Jackson1946 • Physics • US

Dorothy Vaughan is most known for her work as a mathematician at NACA and NASA, and was one of the first African American people to be hired as a mathematician after a new executive order from the Roosevelt administration prohibited discrimination based on race, religion and ethnicity. She was also the first African American woman to supervise staff at the NASA Langley Research Center in Virginia.

Prior to this role, she worked as a mathematics teacher after graduating from Freedom University in 1929 and continued to do so while schools were segregated under Jim Crow laws. While at the Langley Research Center, Vaughan taught herself and her colleagues the programming language of Fortran and specialised in flight path calculations which was crucial during World War 2. She was assigned to a segregated unit which consisted only of African Americans and was also required to use a separate dining room and bathroom.

At aged 60, Vaughan retired from NASA in 1971 after working there for 28 years, and died on November 10 2008 aged 98.

Vaughan’s work is highlighted in Margot Lee Shetterly's book Hidden Figures: The Story of the African-American Women Who Helped Win the Space Race, which was later developed into a film in 2016. She was awarded the Congressional Gold Medal in 2019, 11 years after her death, as well as many other awards and honours.

About Black Voices

Black Voices is an organisation led by technology enthusiasts Zainab, Hassanat, Iona and Saman, who are enraged about the lack of Black, female representation in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering and Maths). They aim to celebrate the achievements of underappreciated, yet crucial women, without whom life as we know it would not be the same. The idea originated from the Black Lives Matter Black Lives Matter Movement and the need for it to exist beyond the social media bubble.
